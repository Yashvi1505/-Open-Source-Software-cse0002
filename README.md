# Part 1: Background — The World Before LibreOffice

## 1.1 StarOffice and the Promise of a Free Alternative

In the late 1990s, Microsoft Office held an iron grip on the desktop software market. It was expensive, tied to Windows, and built on proprietary file formats that locked users in. If you opened a Word document anywhere other than Word, you were taking a risk. For universities, governments, and individuals in the developing world, this was not just inconvenient; it was a structural barrier to participation in the digital economy.

In 1999, Sun Microsystems made a surprising decision. It acquired a German company called **Star Division**, which had built a competing office suite called **StarOffice**, and then open-sourced the entire codebase under the name **OpenOffice.org**.

For the first time, there was a credible, free, cross-platform alternative to Microsoft Office. It could read and write Word, Excel, and PowerPoint files and ran on Linux, Windows, and macOS. Governments and educational institutions adopted it enthusiastically, and by the mid-2000s it had tens of millions of users. By most measures, it was a major open-source success story.

---

## 1.2 The Governance Problem Nobody Wanted to Talk About

Despite its success, a structural problem existed. **Sun Microsystems controlled OpenOffice.org heavily**.

Every developer who wanted to contribute code had to sign a **Joint Copyright Assignment agreement**, meaning their code legally became Sun's property. Sun controlled:

- The release schedule  
- The roadmap  
- Final decisions on features  

Many contributors felt uncomfortable because they were contributing freely while a corporation held the ultimate authority.

However, since Sun was generally a **benign steward**, most contributors accepted the situation. This changed in **2010**.

# Part 2: The Trigger — Oracle Acquires Sun

## 2.1 A Corporate Acquisition That Shook the Open Source World

In **January 2010**, Oracle Corporation acquired Sun Microsystems for **$7.4 billion**.

Oracle was known for:

- Commercial enterprise software  
- Aggressive intellectual property enforcement  

Unlike Sun, Oracle's priorities were **enterprise contracts and revenue generation**. The open-source community quickly became uneasy about the future of OpenOffice.org.

---

## 2.2 What Oracle Did (and Did Not Do)

Oracle did not immediately shut down OpenOffice.org, but several actions caused concern:

- It **kept the copyright assignment requirement**
- It launched a commercial product called **Oracle Open Office**
- Communication with the community became minimal
- Oracle filed a **major lawsuit against Google over Java**

This lawsuit made developers fear that Oracle might use legal rights aggressively.

---

## 2.3 The Community's Question

Inside the developer community, a serious question emerged:

> **Can we trust Oracle with this project?**

The code had not changed.  
The license had not changed.

But **ownership had changed**, and that changed everything.

The copyright assignment model suddenly became a real threat.

# Part 3: The Fork — Creating The Document Foundation

## 3.1 What It Means to Fork

In software development, a **fork** happens when developers copy an existing codebase and begin developing it independently as a new project.

Forking is allowed in open-source software but is rarely done because it:

- Splits the community
- Confuses users
- Divides development effort

However, sometimes a fork becomes necessary.

---

## 3.2 September 2010: The Document Foundation

In **September 2010**, major contributors created a new organization called:

**The Document Foundation**

They launched a new office suite:

**LibreOffice**

Key principles of LibreOffice governance:

- Independent **non-profit foundation**
- **No single company control**
- **No copyright assignment**
- Transparent governance
- Elected leadership

Oracle was invited to donate the **OpenOffice.org brand** to the foundation.  
Oracle refused.

---

## 3.3 Oracle's Response and the Eventual Collapse

Oracle reacted negatively and asked members of the new foundation to leave the OpenOffice.org community council.

Many contributors chose LibreOffice.

Soon after:

- Major Linux distributions (Ubuntu, Fedora, Debian) adopted **LibreOffice**
- Community momentum shifted rapidly

In **2011**, Oracle donated OpenOffice.org to the **Apache Software Foundation**, creating **Apache OpenOffice**.

However, the community had already moved to LibreOffice.

---

# Part 4: The Aftermath — What LibreOffice Became

## 4.1 A Different Governance Model

LibreOffice governance was designed to prevent corporate domination.

Important rules:

- The Document Foundation is a **non-profit organization**
- No company can control **more than one-third of the board**
- Contributors **retain copyright ownership**
- Decisions are made by **elected committees**

Companies like:

- Red Hat
- Canonical
- Collabora

contributed developers without controlling the project.

---

## 4.2 The Numbers

By the early 2020s:

- **200+ million active users**
- Thousands of contributors worldwide
- Default office suite on most **Linux distributions**

Many governments adopted LibreOffice.

Meanwhile:

- **Apache OpenOffice declined**
- Fewer developers
- Rare updates

---

## 4.3 The MySQL Parallel

A similar story happened with **MySQL**, another Sun project acquired by Oracle.

MySQL's original creator **Michael Widenius** feared Oracle control and created a fork called:

**MariaDB**

Today:

- MariaDB is widely used
- Many Linux systems use MariaDB instead of MySQL

The pattern shows how **community trust can collapse when governance fails**.

# Part 5: The Deeper Questions

## 5.1 The Problem of Corporate Open Source

Corporations often open-source software for strategic reasons.

Examples:

- Google → Android
- Facebook → React

These projects help companies attract developers and grow ecosystems.

However, a key question remains:

> What obligations do corporations have to volunteer contributors?

The LibreOffice case suggests companies rarely answer this question clearly.

---

## 5.2 The Limits of Licensing

Open source guarantees four freedoms:

1. Use the software
2. Study the software
3. Modify the software
4. Share the software

But licensing **does not guarantee fair governance**.

A project can be open-source but still controlled by a single company.

---

## 5.3 Who Owns Community Labour?

Open-source volunteers contribute:

- Time
- Skill
- Creativity

But under copyright assignment, a corporation can end up owning their work.

This raises ethical questions about fairness and power imbalance.

---

## 5.4 The Forking Dilemma

Forking protects freedom but comes with major costs:

- Splitting the community
- Losing momentum
- Rebuilding infrastructure

LibreOffice succeeded because **hundreds of skilled developers supported the fork**.

Not every community can do that.

# Part 6: Case Study Assignment

## Section A — Establish the Context

OpenOffice.org began as an open-source alternative to Microsoft Office but contained a structural flaw: **corporate control over community contributions**.

When Oracle acquired Sun, the risk of this structure became clear.

The project was legally open source but **not practically open in governance**.

---

## Section B — The Ethics of Corporate Control

The copyright assignment model raises ethical concerns.

While companies invest resources, volunteer contributors also invest effort and expertise.

A fair system should balance **financial investment and community contribution**.

---

## Section C — The Forking Question

The community could have tried reforming OpenOffice.org internally.

However, evidence suggested Oracle would not change governance.

The fork was difficult but ultimately successful.

LibreOffice became the dominant project.

---

## Section D — Lessons and Generalisation

Two major projects forked after the Oracle acquisition:

- LibreOffice
- MariaDB

This shows how fragile community trust can be.

Good governance should include:

- Non-profit foundations
- No copyright assignment
- Transparent decisions
- Balanced corporate influence

---

## Section E — Reflection

This case shows that open source is **not just about licensing**.

It is also about:

- Governance
- Trust
- Institutional design

LibreOffice succeeded because contributors were willing to defend these principles.

Strong governance structures ensure that open-source communities remain healthy even when leadership changes.
